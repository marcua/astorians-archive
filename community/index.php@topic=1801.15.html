What was it? The weather, the shopping, the diversity—what? What could make anyone feel that it was time to move on. Home ownership? Family? And then what would make anyone want to move to a place where hurricanes are the norm?

I lived (and worked) in Europe. I traveled the globe—more than once. But I always maintained my Astoria apartment. I never required more privacy than an apartment allowed; always found ways to make room for retirement funding; never wanted to have to pay a plumber to fix my leaking sink; always loved my family being scattered around the world so that visits were oh! so very special; my children have glorious memories of Astoria (and I know deep in their hearts will return here to retire) and I still love living here.

I am retired and living on a fixed income (some months barely making ends meet) but I wouldn't change my life one iota—except for health issues.

I've searched my heart before committing to the following . . .

There is nothing, absolutly nothing anyone could offer me that would take me out of Astoria. In the 42 years that I lived in this apartment, neighbors have come and gone. I've made friends of every one of them—and am still in contact with all who have moved on—a few who have great regrets! I've been active in the community—socially and politically. I enjoyed my life.

On a more personal note: when I watched my Dad grow old and frail with Alzheimer's Disease I listened very carefully to what he had to say during many of his 'windows'. He had such regrets about the things he never got to do, say, see. I promised myself I would live life to it's fullest. To see everything I wanted to see. Go everywhere I wanted to go. Be involved. Enjoy.

And I still do.

The rising rents are a big bummer.

However, it's not the transient nature of the neighborhood that makes rents rise, it's just that there is a growing demand and not enough supply.

In other words, if 10 people move out and 10 people move in the rents shouldn't go up.
But when you have 10 people move out, and 100 people looking for places to move into, then there's a lot of demand, so people are willing to pay more to be sure they got a place.

If nobody was willing to move I suppose you could mitigate this a bit, but the movement of people in and out isn't what the underlying factor is, it's the fact that there are more people that want to live here than there are apartments available for them; supply and demand is the underlying problem I think.

Rent controlled apartments are in buildings built before the end of WW II. It was established by the NY State Legislature to keep people from becoming homeless and to prevent landlords from making a financial killing on the rents from people trying to re-establish their lives after the war. The landlords *used* to be able to make no more than 6 1/2 percent profit from their investment in the property annually. That part was written out of the law when Rent Stabilization went into effect.

Only those people who were living in a Rent Controlled apartment at the time of the Rent Stabilization Act, and who continue to reside in those same apartments, are covered under the Rent Control laws. As I recall, Rent Stabilization became effective in the late 60s.

As it stands today the difference is financial. Rent Controlled tenants pay a 7.5 percent increase per year, *every* year. Plus they are also responsible for an annual fuel cost adjustment that gets added to their rent *every* year. Once a Rent Controlled apartment can be legally rented for over $2,000 (and the tenant shows a household income in excess of $175,000 for each of the previous two years) it becomes decontrolled—meaning the landord can get whatever the market will allow. Basically the same provision is there for Stabilized apartments, also. The landlord has absolutely no say in whether or not an apartment is Rent Stabilized. The law is *very* clear on that matter.

Rent Stabilized apartments get from 2–3 percent for a 1 year renewal increase to a 3–6 percent renewal increase for a 2 year lease. These rates are established every two years by the City Council. Rent Controlled apartments have no leases. They are deemed protected under the law.

Any building with less than six dwellings is (generally) exempt from any rent laws.

Each time a tenant moves out, the landlord has the right to do major improvements in controlled *and* stabilized apartments in order to increase the rental cost—even if those improvements are deemed not necessary. The costs for these improvements may be passed on to the new tenant by a formula provided by law. There are also provisions in the law which allow an automatic increase of the basic rental fee each time a new tenant moves into an already Stabilized unit. When a Rent Controlled tenant moves out, the apartment automatically becomes Rent Stabilized, as long as the rental *new* rental is no more than $2,000. There is a formula in the law which allows for the new base rental of that unit.

It is a system designed to make landlords wealthy and middle class families closer and closer to the povery level.
