Astoria is much more dirtier, and noisier recently, no doubt. It breaks my heart to see that it has taken a real step down in the past 12 months.

It doesn't help that so much of Astoria is Sunday night recycling, and with so many holidays on Monday, you often get several weeks of garbage piled up.

Some of my neighbors just don't see the problem, even when rats come out at night and eat the kitchen scaps that falls from their casually tossed bags they shrug or look at me with a blank face.

Not only is there a widening gap in public tastes for cleanliness, but the absentee landlords who don't live in their buildings add to this. Very few use a hose on a regular base to clean their stoops.

Many buildings that had 2 or 3 cans in front 10 years ago now have 10. Twenty years ago it was much much more cleaner, but we had a lot fewer people then. We are going to have lots of fun when 2/3 story buildings are soon to be replaced with 10/12 story buildings.

Can someone look up the statistics that the city publishes? Be interesting to see how Astoria rates with the city, and the rest of Queens.

Another thing to consider is that not only are there more people living here, but people in general (americans in particular) produce a lot mor garbage than we used to. Disposable things are great and useful and convenient and all that, but they do produce a whole lot more trash than things you have to reuse. I spent a month in an Indian village with 4 other americans, I swear that between the five of us we produced more trash than the household we were living in produces in a whole year.

Garbage collection is another interesting question. Every European I know who comes to NY comments on it- why do we just leave piles of trash out on the sidewalks waiting for sanitation to come pick it up? Other cities have dumpsters where the trash is stored until the city comes to pick it up.

And of course I grew up on Roosevelt Island, where all the trash is moved from the apartment buildings to the trash collection facility through underground tunnels.

There are better ways of doing this, but someone has to ask the question loudly enough to be heard.

I think the problem is more with the people than with the number and locations of garbage cans. I was in Tokyo recently, and they have drink vending machines all over the place... but not nearly the same number of trash cans. Often I would be left with an empty drink bottle and be looking for a trash can to put it in. In spite of this, the city has much less litter than NYC does.

People there apparently have the decency to hold onto their trash until they have an appropriate place to put it, as opposed to just throwing it somewhere because they can't be bothered. Look at the subway tracks.. garbage cans are very easily accessible in subway stations, and yet people still throw a bunch of crap onto the tracks. So I get the feeling that simply increasing the number of garbage cans won't make a big difference. The people themselves need an attitude change.
