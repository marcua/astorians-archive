Title: Re: Mayor Proposes a Fee for Driving Into Manhattan
Post by: aquaecny on April 23, 2007, 07:06:02 AM
As far as the congestion pricing works.  It is not for all of Manhattan.  It would be for areas below 86th Street.  Here is the chart from the Mayor's Office.

Zone boundaries
Manhattan below 86th Street, except
• West Street and West Side Highway
• FDR Drive
• Battery Park Underpass
• Queensboro, Williamsburg, Manhattan and Brooklyn Bridges and their approaches.

Hours
6 AM-6 PM, Monday-Friday (no charges on weekends)

Charges
Autos:
$8 daily charge to enter, leave, and move within the zone during charging hours
$4 daily charge for travel only within the zone during charging hours
Trucks:
$21 daily charge to enter, leave, and move within the zone during charging hours
$5.50 daily charge for travel only within the zone during charging hours

Trips bypassing the zone
Drivers do not pay unless they enter the zone. For example, driving from
Brooklyn to the Bronx on the Brooklyn Bridge and FDR Drive would still be free

Toll rebates for E–Z Pass users
E–Z Pass users paying bridge and tunnel tolls to enter the zone will be credited the amount of their round-trip tolls that
day, up to $8. For example, an E–Z Pass driver who now uses the Battery Tunnel to enter and leave Manhattan will pay
no additional charge, because the current round-trip toll they pay is already $8

Exemptions
No charges for:
• Handicapped license plates
• Emergency vehicles and transit buses
• Yellow taxis and livery cabs

Collection technology
At-speed E–Z Pass readers will allow fee collection without slowing vehicles down. Vehicles not equipped with E–Z Pass
will be recorded by cameras and drivers can pay the fee by phone, internet, or at participating retailers within 48 hours.

Revenues
All net revenues will be dedicated 100% to transportation investments through the SMART Financing Authority

Operating Entity
NYC Department of Transportation will control the system, which will be built and maintained by a contractor
yet to be selected

One of the interesting subtleties about the mayor's plan has to do with tolls. Currently you pay $8 or whatever to get into the city from some bridges/tunnels but not others. With congestion pricing, any toll you pay is *deducted* from your fee for entering the city. So if you live in Jersey and enter over the GWB, you'll end up paying nothing additional under this plan. But if you live in Queens and take the 59th St bridge into the city, your commute will no longer be free. It'll shift traffic from the current free routes into the city to the toll-based routes. Which authorities get revenue from the tolls? I bet that this feature of the plan will reduce their concerns...

Title: Re: Mayor Proposes a Fee for Driving Into Manhattan
Post by: aquaecny on April 23, 2007, 06:29:25 PM
Quote from: Harlan on April 23, 2007, 09:35:30 AM
Which authorities get revenue from the tolls? I bet that this feature of the plan will reduce their concerns...

As far as I know, the tolls of any route that connect New York and New Jersey go to the PORT AUTHORITY as revenue.  This includes the George Washington Bridge, Lincoln and Holland Tunnels, and the Bayonne and Goethals Bridges.

The tolls of any route fully within New York State go to the MTA as revenue.  These routes include Triborough Bridge, Throgs Neck Bridge, Verrazano-Narrows Bridge, Bronx-Whitestone Bridge, Henry Hudson Bridge, Marine Parkway Gil Hodges Memorial Bridge (to Rockaways), Cross Bay Veterans Memorial Bridge (to Rockaways), Brooklyn Battery Tunnel, and the Queens Midtown Tunnel.  I think at one time they even received the tolls from the Tappan Zee Bridge, but I may be incorrect on this one.  It may be just the NYS Thruway Authority that gets this money.

And that leaves the revenues from the Congestion Fee going to the DoT through a newly created Sustainable Mobility and Regional Transportation (SMART) Financing Authority.

Make it more expensive for companies to do business in a place, they'll move. Make it more expensive for people to live in a place, they'll move. People think, incorrectly, that New York City is somehow, intrinsically, the best city in the world and destined to remain among the most desirable in which to live and work. Every time the cost of living here increases, other cities become more appealing to current businesses and new businesses and existing and prospective residents. New York City has recently become a city that more young people are leaving then coming to. "Making it" here has become excessively burdensome, so people are making it elsewhere.

Bloomberg's real estate tax hikes have helped drive up rents and this new congestion fee thing, however well intentioned, is likely to hurt more than help. The expected consequence will be fewer cars in the city. The unintended but very real consequence will be an acceleration of capital and population flight from NYC and a higher cost of living per capita for those of us who remain.

I know people who are hardly "wealthy" for whom their cars are integral to their small businesses. Actors, caterers, guys who run street corner restaurants, independent consultants, teachers who volunteer to bring students to different locations for extracurricular activities... this law makes their lives more difficult, their products more expensive (harder to sell)... so they'll make different choices, which will have detrimental ripple effects, as well.

What would I suggest? Break the monopoly on public transportation and cab medallions. Allow a market to develop in mass commuting. Competition leads to a more efficient use of resources, more options for commuters to choose among, lower prices over time. The government's monopoly on roads and mass transit has stifled innovation in the development and implementation of options for people seeking a way into and around NYC. If I wanted to start a company in NYC that competed directly with cabs and utilized a fleet of hybrid cars, how far would I get?

If you still believe the government should be involved in mass transit and needs more money to spend on deploying better options, maybe you should advocate, vociferously, that before the city finds a new way to bleed taxpayers it should cease and desist the hundreds of millions of dollars spent on subsidies to businesses and real estate developers and divert those funds to new transit development. I still think the answer lies elsewhere, but this would be easier for me to swallow than another attack on people's hard earned dollars. Just a thought.

That was an interesting article Frappe.  I think the stats generally speak for themselved in in it, but my favorite was this "Drivers make up just 16 percent of all the commuters who work in this dense section of Manhattan".  Most people that live in NYC do not own a car, this plan benefits the majority, and is being held up by a vocal, and in most case wealthier, majority.

I have a hard time believing that there is this flood of low income people who pay to maintain a car, pay for gas to commute daily, pay for parking on a daily basis and pay car insurance.  I think most people that rely on cars on a daily basis tend to be wealthier, or at worst middle class.  Many people say, "Gosh if they put this thru, I just won't drive into the city anymore, so there", as if that's some sort of punishment to the city.  Thats the point, the charge is supposed to act as a deterrent, not a toll.

One last anecdotal story about those "long" mass transit commute times from places like Long Island.  My current boss lives near Jones Beach.  He bikes, everyday, to the LIRR station there, and takes the train into the city, where he switches to the subway and rides that the rest of the way to SoHo (Spring and 6th Ave) where we currently work.  Takes him 45 minutes.  I live a 5 minute walk to Astoria Blvd station and take it to Soho as well, no transfers (I walk from Prince to 6th Ave) and it takes me an hour.  Long transit my ass.  Plus on the ride home he has drinks with his commuting buddies.

(Moving a discussion over from the events board...)

Quote from: BigBryan on June 07, 2007, 11:53:09 PM
My point is that any change in congestion would be negligible at best.

And this is entirely possible, and is a great point to bring up.

About 10 years ago, gasoline was shockingly cheap, less than $1 a gallon. Now it's over $3 a gallon. Back then, if you told people that gas was going to be as expensive as it is, they would have told you that they would drive less, buy smaller cars, etc. But how much effect has the increase in prices really had? Not very much. Fewer people drive across the country, I guess, and SUV sales are down (but big pickup sales are up), but everyone still drives to work, and it's not like the streets of New York and Los Angeles are empty.

Financial incentives work really well for businesses, who have penny-pinching managers trying to make the most money possible, and also have the ability to invest in new methods to reduce long-term costs. They seem to work considerably less well for individual people and families.

So, will congestion pricing actually work, like it mostly did in London? Or will it just be seen as an annoyance by drivers who will just glower and pay the fee?

(Presumably it will work to improve traffic flow over the QBB, as the Midtown tunnel will immediately become less expensive.)
